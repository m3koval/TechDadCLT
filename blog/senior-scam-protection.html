How Scammers Target Seniors Online —
And What to Do About It

By Mikhail · TechDad CLT 5 minute read For adult children of aging parents
$3.4B
Lost by seniors to online fraud in 2023 (FBI)
#1
Age group targeted by tech support and investment fraud
1 in 6
Older adults experience some form of elder fraud annually

The FBI's 2023 Elder Fraud Report found that Americans over 60 lost more than $3.4 billion to internet crime — more than any other age group, and a number that almost certainly undercounts reality because most cases go unreported out of embarrassment or confusion.

This isn't about seniors being "gullible." These scams are sophisticated, professionally executed, and specifically designed to exploit the habits and trust patterns of people who grew up before the internet. And unlike younger generations who grew up learning to be skeptical of suspicious emails, many seniors never developed those instincts — because they didn't need to.

Here's what these scams look like in practice, and what you can actually do about it.

The 4 scams that cause the most damage

Scam #1

Government impersonation (IRS, Medicare, Social Security)

A caller claims to be from the IRS, Medicare, or Social Security Administration and tells your parent they owe money, their benefits are being suspended, or there's been suspicious activity on their account. The caller is urgent, authoritative, and threatening — sometimes saying law enforcement will be involved if they don't pay immediately.

The payment method is always a red flag: gift cards (iTunes, Google Play, Amazon), wire transfer, or cryptocurrency. Real government agencies never demand these.

"This is Agent David Harris from the IRS. We have issued a warrant for your arrest due to tax fraud. To avoid immediate arrest, you must pay $1,847 in Google Play cards within the next two hours."
Scam #2

Tech support fraud

A pop-up appears on their computer warning that it's been infected with a virus and to "Call Microsoft immediately" at a provided phone number. Or they receive a call from someone claiming to be from Apple or Microsoft saying their account has been compromised.

The scammer talks them into installing remote access software (like AnyDesk or TeamViewer), then appears to "fix" the problem while actually accessing their bank account, stealing saved passwords, or locking the computer and demanding payment to unlock it.

"I'm calling from the Windows Security Team. We've detected suspicious activity originating from your computer. I need you to download this program so I can remotely fix the issue before your files are permanently deleted."
Scam #3

Grandparent scam

They receive a frantic call from someone pretending to be a grandchild (or a lawyer or police officer representing one) saying the grandchild is in trouble — arrested, in a hospital, in a car accident abroad — and needs money immediately. The caller begs them not to tell the parents because they don't want them to worry.

With AI voice cloning now widely accessible, scammers can sometimes generate a convincing imitation of a real family member's voice from just a few seconds of audio found on social media.

"Grandma, it's me — I'm in trouble. I got into an accident in Mexico and I need $3,000 to get out. Please don't tell Mom and Dad. My lawyer will tell you how to send it."
Scam #4

Romance and friendship scams

A stranger reaches out on Facebook, a dating app, or even via a wrong-number text. They're warm, attentive, and interested. Over weeks or months, they build a real relationship — and then a crisis strikes: a medical emergency, a business deal gone wrong, a flight they can't afford. They just need a little help.

The average loss in a romance scam is over $10,000. Many victims lose their life savings. The emotional manipulation makes these especially painful — and victims are often too ashamed to report them.

Warning signs your parent may be at risk

Most seniors won't volunteer that they've been scammed. Watch for these signs:

  • Unusual bank transfers, especially to unfamiliar accounts or in round numbers
  • Purchasing gift cards (any kind) without an obvious recipient
  • Secretive or anxious behavior around their phone or computer
  • References to a new "friend" online who they've never met in person
  • Pop-ups on their computer they don't know how to close
  • Any third-party software installed they can't explain (remote access tools especially)
  • Reluctance to talk about their finances or online activity
🚨
If a scam has already happened
Don't wait. Contact their bank immediately to freeze or reverse transactions if possible. Report to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center at ic3.gov and the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov. Contact Adult Protective Services if you're concerned about ongoing exploitation.

5 concrete steps to protect a parent today

  • Have the conversation — directly, without shame. Tell them exactly what these scams look like. Use the examples above. Most seniors haven't heard the specifics. Explain that the scammers are professional, and that being fooled does not mean being stupid. Ask them to call you before sending money to anyone for any reason.
  • Set up a call-screening app on their phone. On iPhone, enable "Silence Unknown Callers" in Settings → Phone. On Android, enable Google's built-in call screening. This alone eliminates a huge percentage of scam call attempts before they can speak to your parent.
  • Set up a secure, simple email account with strong filtering. If they use Gmail, turn on Gmail's enhanced spam filtering and enable two-factor authentication using a simple method like a backup phone number. Many phishing emails are caught automatically — but only if spam filters are actually enabled.
  • Check their computer for unfamiliar software. Look for remote access tools (AnyDesk, TeamViewer, LogMeIn) they can't explain, browser extensions they didn't install, and any security software they "bought" from a phone call. Remove anything suspicious.
  • Review their Facebook privacy settings. Set their profile to visible to Friends only. Turn off the ability for strangers to send them messages. Remove their phone number and email from their public profile. These details are mined by scammers to personalize their approach.
